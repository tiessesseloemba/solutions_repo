Escape Velocities and Cosmic Velocities

Motivation

The concept of escape velocity is fundamental in astrophysics and space exploration. It determines the minimum speed required for an object to break free from a celestial body's gravitational pull.

Expanding on this, the first, second, and third cosmic velocities define the speed thresholds needed for stable orbit, escape from a planet, and even escape from an entire star system. These principles are essential for launching satellites, interplanetary missions, and potential future interstellar travel.

Definitions of Cosmic Velocities

  1. First Cosmic Velocity (Orbital Velocity, \(v_1\))

  2. The minimum speed needed for an object to stay in a stable circular orbit around a celestial body without propulsion.

  3. Derived from balancing gravitational force and centripetal force:

    $$ v_1 = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}} $$

  4. Example: A satellite in low Earth orbit (LEO) must travel at ~7.91 km/s.

  5. Second Cosmic Velocity (Escape Velocity, \(v_2\))

  6. The speed required to escape completely from a planetâ€™s gravitational influence.

  7. Derived from energy conservation (gravitational potential energy vs. kinetic energy):

    $$ v_2 = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}} $$

  8. It is \(\sqrt{2}\) times the first cosmic velocity.

  9. Example:

    On Earth, \(v_2 \approx 11.2\) km/s, meaning a rocket must reach this speed to leave Earth without further propulsion.

  10. Third Cosmic Velocity (Stellar Escape Velocity, \(v_3\))

  11. The speed needed to escape the gravitational pull of the entire solar system (or another star system).

  12. Depends on both the Sunâ€™s gravity and the planetâ€™s position in its orbit:

    $$ v_3 = \sqrt{v_2^2 + v_s^2} $$

    where \(v_s\) is the planetâ€™s orbital velocity around the Sun.

  13. Example:

    To leave the Solar System from Earthâ€™s orbit, \(v_3 \approx 42.1\) km/s.

Space Exploration Applications

  • Launching satellites: The first cosmic velocity determines the speed needed for stable orbits (e.g., ISS at ~7.66 km/s).

  • Interplanetary missions: The second cosmic velocity is crucial for rockets escaping planetary gravity (e.g., Apollo missions).

  • Leaving the Solar System: Probes like Voyager 1 & 2 reached the third cosmic velocity to exit the Solar System.
